Experiment: Testing the Alkalinity of Water

Introduction

Alkalinity in water is the water's capacity to resist changes in pH. It is mainly due to the presence of bicarbonates, carbonates, and hydroxides. Testing water alkalinity helps us understand its buffering capacity, which is important for water quality in natural bodies and aquariums.

Materials Needed

  • Water sample (tap water, lake water, etc.)
  • Burette or pipette
  • pH indicator (such as phenolphthalein)
  • 0.02 N sulfuric acid solution
  • Beakers
  • pH meter or pH paper
  • Stirring rod

Procedure

Step 1: Take a 100 ml sample of the water to be tested in a beaker.
Step 2: Add a few drops of phenolphthalein indicator to the water sample. If the solution turns pink, it indicates the presence of hydroxides or carbonates.
Step 3: Fill a burette with 0.02 N sulfuric acid solution.
Step 4: Carefully add sulfuric acid to the water sample, drop by drop, while stirring, until the pink color just disappears. Note the volume of sulfuric acid used.
Step 5: If there was no pink color initially, add a few drops of methyl orange indicator to the solution, which will turn yellow.
Step 6: Continue to add sulfuric acid until the solution changes to orange or red. Record the total amount of acid used.

Conclusion

The amount of sulfuric acid required to reach the endpoint can be used to calculate the water's alkalinity. By measuring alkalinity, we can determine the buffering capacity of the water, which is vital for maintaining stable pH levels in aquatic environments.

Determination of Water Hardness by EDTA Method

Determining Hardness of Water by EDTA Method

Introduction

Water hardness is primarily caused by the presence of calcium (Ca2+) and magnesium (Mg2+) ions. The EDTA (Ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid) titration method is a common and effective way to determine the hardness of water. This method involves using EDTA as a complexing agent that binds with calcium and magnesium ions.

Materials Required

  • Water sample
  • EDTA solution (0.01 M)
  • Eriochrome Black T indicator
  • Ammonia buffer solution (NH3-NH4Cl)
  • Burette, pipette, and conical flask
  • Distilled water

Procedure

  1. Fill the burette with the standard EDTA solution.
  2. Take 50 mL of the water sample in a conical flask.
  3. Add 1-2 mL of ammonia buffer solution to maintain a pH of around 10.
  4. Add a few drops of Eriochrome Black T indicator. The solution will turn wine-red if hardness ions are present.
  5. Titrate the solution with EDTA until the color changes from wine-red to blue, indicating the endpoint.

Calculation

The hardness of water (in ppm) can be calculated using the formula:

Hardness (ppm) = (Volume of EDTA used) × (Molarity of EDTA) × (1000) / (Volume of water sample in mL)

Example Calculation

If 15 mL of 0.01 M EDTA solution was required to reach the endpoint for a 50 mL water sample:

Hardness (ppm) = (15 mL) × (0.01 M) × (1000) / (50 mL) = 300 ppm

Conclusion

This method provides a reliable measure of the total hardness of water, which includes both calcium and magnesium ions. Regular testing of water hardness is important for industries and households to ensure water quality.
