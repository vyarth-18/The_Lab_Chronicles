TITRATION OF STRONG ACID VS. STRONG BASE USING A CONDUCTIVITY METER AND TO DETERMINE THE STRENGTH OF THE ACID

Prior Concepts:

Conductance, resistance, variation in conductance w.r.t concentration. Weak and strong acids-bases.

New Concepts:

Proposition 1: The principle of conductometric titration is based on the fact that during titration, one of the ion is replaced by the other and invariably these two ions differ in the ionic conductance with the result that conductivity of the solution varies during the titration.

Proposition 2: The equivalence point is located graphically by plotting change in conductance as function of the volume of titrant is added.

Information Input:

1. When the mixture of CH3COOH and HCL is titrated against NaOH,HCL being the strong acid will neutralise first followed by CH3COOH.

2. Neutralisation of CH3COOH(weak acid) will commence only after the complete neutralisation of HCL.

3. The Conductance titration curve is marked by two breaks, the first one corresponds to neutralisation of HCL and second one to that of CH3COOH.

4. Let V1 and V2 mL be the volume of alkali corresponding to first and second breaks respectively.

V1 mL of NaOH=HCL , V2 mL of NaOH=HCL+CH3COOH

Hence,mL of NaOH=CH3COOH

Stepwise procedure:

1. Clean all the apparatus with distilled water.

2. Fill the burette with 0.01 N NaOH solution. Place the conductivity cell in distilled water and adjust the display to pH with calibration knob.

3. Take a beaker and add 30 mL of HCL.

4. Titrate the mixture against 0.1 N NaOH solution.

5. Now measure initial conductance of solution.

6. Then add 0.1 mL of NaOH every time into the solution and stir well each time

7. Note down conductance values till the conductance values decreases and increases considerably.

8. Plot a graph of conductance vs. mL of NaOH added.

Experiment: Measuring Alkalinity of Water Using Acid

Introduction

Alkalinity is a measure of the water's capacity to neutralize acids, primarily due to the presence of bicarbonates, carbonates, and hydroxides. This experiment uses an acid titration method to determine the alkalinity of a water sample.

Materials Needed

  • Water sample (tap water, river water, etc.)
  • Burette or pipette
  • 0.02 N sulfuric acid solution
  • Phenolphthalein indicator
  • Methyl orange indicator
  • Beakers (100 ml)
  • Stirring rod
  • pH meter or pH strips (optional)

Procedure

Step 1: Take a 100 ml water sample in a clean beaker.
Step 2: Add 3-4 drops of phenolphthalein indicator to the sample. If the solution turns pink, it indicates the presence of hydroxides and carbonates.
Step 3: Using a burette, add 0.02 N sulfuric acid drop by drop while gently stirring the solution.
Step 4: Continue adding the acid until the pink color disappears. Note the volume of acid used (this represents the phenolphthalein alkalinity).
Step 5: Add 3-4 drops of methyl orange indicator to the same sample. The solution should turn yellow.
Step 6: Continue titrating with sulfuric acid until the solution changes to a red or orange color. Record the total volume of acid used (this represents the total alkalinity).

Calculating Alkalinity

The alkalinity of the water sample can be calculated using the formula:

Alkalinity (mg/L as CaCO3) = (Volume of acid used in ml) × (Normality of acid) × 50,000 / (Volume of water sample in ml)

This result indicates the water's ability to neutralize acids, which is important for maintaining pH balance in aquatic environments and water treatment processes.

Conclusion

The titration method using a strong acid (such as sulfuric acid) allows us to determine the alkalinity of a water sample. Higher alkalinity indicates a greater capacity of the water to buffer pH changes, which is critical for ecosystems and industrial applications.
